Project Overview

From the previous extinction module, were pretty sure that the sixth mass extinction is around the corner. According to its definition, extinction usually begins with the loss in abundance of individuals that can result in compositional and functional changes of ecosystems. This assignment would be based on the research done by Kenneth V. Rosenberg, Decline of the North American avifauna. We would use the data his team once used and answer the following three questions: 1. Is the population of birds really declining? To what extent? 2. If the answer to the first question is yes, is every famliy of birds encountering the same decline? In other words, is every species being affected equally by this changing environment? 3. Question 2: Is the decline of populations even among all speices? Are there certain species that experience more effect than others?

Lets dig deeper based on what weve done in question 1. We would group the data by family to make everything easy to read. To get the proportion of loss, we divide the mean_loss by the total mean_loss instead of 3.2 billiion, which is the amount given in Rosenbergs paper.

family_loss <- simple_data %>% 
  group_by(Family)%>%
  summarize(mean_loss = mean(Loss_med), number=n()) %>% 
  filter(mean_loss > 0) %>% 
  mutate(proportion_of_loss = mean_loss/sum(mean_loss)) %>%
  arrange(desc(proportion_of_loss))

There are some families that experience population gains. Lets list them out. As what we did before for the family_loss, we divide the mean_gain by the total gains, calculated by sum(mean_gain).

family_gain <- simple_data %>% 
  group_by(Family)%>%
  summarize(mean_loss = mean(Loss_med), number=n()) %>% 
  filter(mean_loss < 0) %>% 
  mutate(mean_gain = -mean_loss, proportion_of_gain = mean_gain/sum(mean_gain)) %>%

Question 3: Whats the effect of declining of birds on other related species?

We further explore the relationship between birds and insects.

In our dataset, theres one column AI that identifies species in the Aerial Insectivore group, birds that capture flying insects while in flight.

AI <- simple_data  %>% 
  group_by(AI) %>%

From the article Plummeting insect numbers threaten collapse of nature, we surprisingly find that more than 40% of insect species are declining and a third are endangered. The rate of extinction is eight times faster than that of mammals, birds and reptiles. The total mass of insects is falling by a precipitous 2.5% a year, according to the best data available, suggesting they could vanish within a century. This contradicts with our findings from the extinction module, where we found that the rate of decline for birds is higher than that of insects. Therefore, we requires further investigation with more precise data to learn about the relationship between birds and insects.
